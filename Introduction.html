Blue Club Dahlonega#

by robb sinn

Blue Club Dahlonega presents a bidding system for bridge recalling the legendary strong \(1\clubsuit\) system developed and deployed in top tier competitions by the Italian national team. We leverage the aggressive vibe of the \(1\clubsuit\) bid and the 4-card major openings while minimizing the disruptive chaos that oppoents can inflict with competitive bidding.

Italian Blue Club

Benito Garozzo led the development of the Blue Club system which gained popularity when first used to devastating effect by the Italian national team in the 1960’s. Giorgio Belladonna and Pietro Forquet were great Italian national team players who helped with the development, as well, determining via high level competition how best to implement the idens of Arturo Franco, the architect of the strong club system used by the Italian Blue Team.

Quick and Accurate Early Bidding#

Blue Club quickly deploys an accurate picture of hand strength and shape with first and second bids that:

  • Limit the power of our holdings to a specific range of points, and

  • Describe distributional features of our hand.

In this game of suits, shape cannot be ignored. We must describe both points strength and our distribution with early bidding. Specific features of Blue Club assist us:

  1. The strong 1\(\clubsuit\) opening signals a great hand: 17+ points.

  2. Canape bidding alerts partner to a two-suiter with a 54xx shape or better.

  3. Opening 4-Card majors adds to the aggression and preemptory vibe.

The strong 1\(\clubsuit\) signals a big hand, yet it also limits the opening range for level-one suit bids. With 1\(\heartsuit\) restricting opener to 12-16 points, responder’s life becomes much easier. Opening 4-card majors with a canape approach allows Blue Club players show two-suiters without promising additional values. Since the first suit bid can contain as few as 4 cards, a 2-suited hand can be 54xx or stronger.

Tip

Arturo Franco believed that bridge rather than being a game of points is, instead, a game of suits. The shape of the hands and the fit between partners is hugely valuable in addition to the combined point count.

The Blue Club partnerships enjoy opening with \(1\textcolor{red}{\heartsuit}\) and rebidding \(2\textcolor{red}{\diamondsuit}\). Partner knows the point count is 12-16 as the canape signals that we have at least 4 hearts and 5 or more diamonds. By opening a 4-card major followed by a canape into a second suit, opener provides responder two chances to find a superfit in trumps. Opponents can only enter the bidding by overcalling the hearts opener. Overcalling a major suit opening can be difficult and places pressure directly upon the opponents in the first round.

Resources#

Blue Club Dahlonega provides a strong club system for beginners and leverages the work of others. The Fiori Napoletano describes a modern Italian canape system quite similar to the original Blue Team Club. The linked text has great advice for dealing with interference.

Alex Knox authored Blue Club, Calgary Casual Style in 1999 which provides an informative introduction to a strong club system for the beginner-to-intermediate player. Additional help guides and tutorials can be found by searching for Polish Club, Precision Club, Moscito, or Revision Club (an updated version of Precision).

Opponents and Interference#

The bridge world recognized the power of the Italians’ strong club bidding and reacted with countermeasures. An overcall of the \(1\clubsuit\) opening bid uses up bidding space needed to place the perfect contract. Weak 2 and Weak 3 overcalls abound with some opponents overcalling with almost nothing.

How does Blue Club Dahlonega navigate competetive bidding? Our bids still specificy a limitted point-range for our hands, and we need to describe the distributional qualities of our hands. Opponents will refuse to sit quietly and watch that happen. Some descendants of Blue Club have become quite artificial in response to the aggressive competitive bidding deployed by the opposition.

Blue Club Dahlonega accepts the competetive bidding challege with grace yet develops ways through the chaos that allow the bidding to be as natural and informative to partner as possible.

Traditional Bidding#

High card point count (HCP) is classically determined in 4321 fashion created by Milton Work and popularized by the lgegendary player and writer Charles Goren. Traditionally, we determine hand strength for bridge as follows:

Hand Strength in High Card Points
Work Points: HCP

  • Aces: 4 points

  • Kings: 3 points

  • Queens: 2 points

  • Jacks: 1 point

Values per Contract
Points Needed for Game or Slam

  • Game in No Trump: 25 points

  • Game in a suit contract: 26 points

  • Small slam: 33 points

  • Grand slam: 37 points

Because exactly 40 HCP must be distributed amongst four players, bridge partnerships can count on certain HCP minimums leading to winning outcomes (as shown above). Yet, High Card Points alone do not tell the full story, and many different ways are used to deal with distributional hands. A bridge partnership requires a 4-4 fit in trumps to make game, but a superfit in trumps such as 6-5 or 7-4 may lead to a team HCP of 22 - 23 being successful.

A superfit is a trump suit 9 total cards, or more. At a superfit of 10, we can double DP due to the additional trick-taking power of the combined hands.

The variations seem limitless. However, for this guide, we will use the traditional HCP count altered by distributional points (DP). This method provides both simplicity and accuracy. As the partnership develops and improves, additional complexity may useful or even necessary.

Advantages of Blue Club#

Strong club systems deliver more information to partner more quickly than Standard American (SAYC) and other modern systems – provided there’s no interference. For big hands, the strong club opening delivers news of our 17 or more HCP immediately. Blue Club opening bids allow bidding space to explore features of our holdings and potential trump fit. Canape bidding shows our strongest suit second. Partner knows we must have at least 9 cards in our top two suits, 4 or more in the first suit bid and 5 or more in the second. Every bridge partership is seeking a super-fit of 9 or more trumps as it enhances the distributional qualities of the hands and makes the partnership holdings more powerful. Canape bidding helps the partership identify where super-fit may be possible.

Current Standard American bridge bidding and 2 over 1 systems require opening bids in a major suit to be 13-21 HCP with 5 or more of the suit bid. Alternatively, the constraints are tighter in Blue Club where we open the top of this range with our strong 1\(\clubsuit\). Opening 4-card majors and using the Rule of 20, a Blue Club player opens many more hands with 1\(\heartsuit\) and 1\(\spadesuit\) than typical players. This adds a preemptive style to the system but also adds accuracy. When our Blue Club partner opens with 1\(\diamondsuit\), 1\(\heartsuit\) and 1\(\spadesuit\), she has narrowed the possible range to 12 - 16 HCP which makes follow-on bidding simpler and more effective.

Blue Club has no natural 1\(\clubsuit\) opening bid which can be quite a disadvantage. The advantage of the strong club opening bid together with opening 4-card majors and using canape-style follow-on bids, Blue Club players have a great deal of flexibility that overcomes many of the disadvantages.

Natural Bidding Systems#

Relays, transfer bids, inverted minors, and Drury bids exemplify how unnatural bids in modern bridge can be. Artificial bids may result in misunderstandings. The more natural our bidding, the more likely partner will know how to respond. Seeking accuracy for every eventuality with artificial bids results in a problem of memory – too much information to remember easily when playing.

Many bridge hands are thorny and do provide challenges to natural bidding. Hence, we need some conventions. In this system we will use the following conventions:

  • Stayman

  • Splinters

  • Cue Bids

  • Roman Key Card Blackwood (1430)

Note that Stayman in Blue Club may need a slightly different treament due to the fact we can open 4-card majors. Alex Knox has a good treament: Blue Club Calgary, pp. 49-50. Blue Club Dahlonega remains as natural as possible so that the partnership avoids tricking itself into nonsense contracts.

Hand Evaluation#

Most modern bridge players have a way to combine distributional points (DP) to the traditional High Card Points (HCP) where:

\[\text{Total Points} = \text{HCP} + \text{DP}\]

While some methods are quite sophisticated, Blue Club Dahlonega chooses simplicity instead. The cost in effectivenss is quite minimal.

Distributional Points#

The DP for Blue Club Dahlonega will remain easy to count but focused on key ideas:

Distributional Points: DP

  • Add 1 DP for every card in a long suit beyond 4.

  • Add 2 DP for a void suit.

  • Add 1 DP for a singleton.

Strength of Holdings#

To further evaluate the distributional properties of a hand, Blue Club Dahlonega includes three additional bridge ideas used widely by modern players and other systems:

Rule of 20

Provided the HCP of the hand plus the number of cards in our 2 longest suits is at least 20, we can confidently open with 1\(\clubsuit\), 1\(\heartsuit\) or 1\(\spadesuit\). The Rule of 20 helps determine when a bridge hand is strong enough be opened by standard level-one suit bid.

Controls

Control of suit indicates a singleton, a void suit, or a suit holding like Kx. First round control indicates we have the first winner in the suit: a void or singleton Ace. Second round control indicates we have the second winner in the suit such as Kx or a small singleton.

Bad Shape

The worst hands in bridge have a 4333 distribution. They play worse than the HCP would indicate. We will discount by one HCP all hands with a 4-3-3-3 shape and refuse to open them unless they pass the Rule of 20.
